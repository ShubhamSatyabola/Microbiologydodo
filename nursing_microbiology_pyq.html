Structures & Classification of Microbes
Unit I · Long Answer
▾

Definition

  • Microorganisms (microbes) are living entities too small to be seen by the naked eye (<0.1 mm)
  • Studied using microscopes; include bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, algae, and prions

Classification of Microbes

GroupCell TypeExamples
BacteriaProkaryoteE. coli, Staph, Strep
FungiEukaryoteCandida, Aspergillus
ProtozoaEukaryotePlasmodium, Entamoeba
AlgaeEukaryoteChlorella
VirusesAcellularHIV, Influenza
PrionsProtein onlyCJD agent

Bacterial Structure

  • Cell wall: Rigid outer layer; composed of peptidoglycan; determines Gram reaction
  • Cell membrane: Semi-permeable phospholipid bilayer; site of enzymes and transport
  • Cytoplasm: Contains ribosomes (70S), enzymes, inclusions
  • Nucleoid: Circular DNA — no membrane-bound nucleus
  • Plasmids: Extrachromosomal circular DNA; carries resistance genes
  • Capsule: Polysaccharide slime layer; anti-phagocytic virulence factor
  • Flagella: Protein appendages for motility (flagellin protein)
  • Pili/Fimbriae: Attachment organelles; sex pilus for conjugation
  • Spores (endospores): Resistant dormant structures — Bacillus, Clostridium
  • Ribosomes: 70S (50S + 30S) — target of many antibiotics
  • Mesosome: Infolding of membrane — aids in cell division

Classification by Shape (Morphology)

  • Cocci: Spherical — Staphylococcus (grape clusters), Streptococcus (chains), Diplococci (pairs)
  • Bacilli: Rod-shaped — E. coli, Bacillus anthracis
  • Spirilla: Rigid spiral — Spirillum
  • Spirochaetes: Flexible spiral — Treponema, Leptospira
  • Vibrio: Comma-shaped — Vibrio cholerae
  • Coccobacilli: Intermediate — Haemophilus influenzae

Classification by Gram Stain

  • Gram Positive (+): Thick peptidoglycan wall; retain crystal violet; appear purple — Staph, Strep, Bacillus
  • Gram Negative (−): Thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane; decolorized; appear red — E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas
  • Acid-Fast: Waxy cell wall (mycolic acid); resist decolorization — Mycobacterium

Classification by Oxygen Requirement

  • Obligate aerobe: Needs O₂ — Mycobacterium TB
  • Obligate anaerobe: Dies in O₂ — Clostridium
  • Facultative anaerobe: Grows with or without O₂ — E. coli
  • Microaerophile: Needs small O₂ — Helicobacter pylori
  • Aerotolerant: Survives O₂ but doesn't use it — Lactobacillus
🧠 Mnemonic — Bacterial Structures: "Some People Can Find Naked Cell Parts" S=Spore, P=Pili, C=Capsule, F=Flagella, N=Nucleoid, C=Cell wall, P=Plasmid

Motility of Bacteria: Types & Significance
Unit I · Short/Long Answer
▾

Definition

  • Motility = the ability of bacteria to move independently in a liquid medium
  • Not all bacteria are motile; it is a key characteristic for identification

Types of Motility

  • 1. Flagellar Motility (Swimming): Most common; flagella rotate like propellers; driven by proton motive force
  • 2. Gliding Motility: Slow movement over surfaces without flagella — Myxobacteria, Cytophaga
  • 3. Spirochaetal Motility (Corkscrew): Axial filaments (endoflagella) between inner and outer membranes — Treponema, Borrelia
  • 4. Swarming Motility: Coordinated group movement on solid surfaces — Proteus mirabilis
  • 5. Twitching Motility: Jerky movement via retractile pili — Pseudomonas aeruginosa

Types by Flagella Arrangement

  • Atrichous: No flagella — non-motile — Klebsiella
  • Monotrichous: Single polar flagellum — Vibrio cholerae
  • Lophotrichous: Tufts at one or both poles — Spirillum
  • Amphitrichous: Flagella at both ends
  • Peritrichous: Flagella all around — E. coli, Salmonella

Significance of Motility

  • Pathogenicity: Enables bacteria to reach target tissues; spread infection (e.g., H. pylori penetrates gastric mucus)
  • Chemotaxis: Movement toward nutrients or away from toxins
  • Identification: Hanging drop / stab culture motility tests help identify organisms
  • Biofilm formation: Motility essential for initial colonization of surfaces
  • Dissemination of infection: Flagellated organisms spread faster in host tissues
  • Antigenicity: H-antigen of flagella used in serotyping (e.g., Salmonella typhi H antigen)

Detection of Motility

  • Hanging drop preparation: Direct microscopy; observe Brownian vs true motility
  • Semisolid agar stab: Motile = diffuse turbidity; non-motile = growth only along stab line
  • Electron microscopy: Visualize flagella structure
🧠 Flagella types: "A Monkey Loves Playing Around" A=Atrichous, M=Monotrichous, L=Lophotrichous, P=Peritrichous, A=Amphitrichous

Define Microbiology & Its Essence in Nursing
Unit I · Definition + Application
▾

Definition

  • Microbiology = study of microorganisms (micro = small; bios = life; logos = study)
  • Includes study of bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, algae, and prions
  • Father of Microbiology: Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (first microscope, 1676)
  • Louis Pasteur — Germ theory of disease; Robert Koch — Koch's postulates

Branches of Microbiology

  • Bacteriology: Study of bacteria
  • Virology: Study of viruses
  • Mycology: Study of fungi
  • Parasitology: Study of parasites
  • Immunology: Study of immune system
  • Medical/Clinical Microbiology: Applied to patient care

Essence in Nursing Practice

  • Infection Prevention & Control: Knowledge of pathogens guides proper hand hygiene, PPE use, and Standard Precautions
  • Aseptic Technique: Understanding sterility prevents HAIs during invasive procedures
  • Specimen Collection: Correct collection, labeling, and transport of clinical specimens
  • Drug Administration: Understanding antibiotic mechanism of action and resistance ensures proper use
  • Patient Education: Nurse educates patients on disease transmission and prevention
  • Wound Care: Identification of wound infection and appropriate dressing change
  • Sterilization & Disinfection: Nurses implement appropriate methods for instruments, environment, and skin
  • Epidemiology: Tracking of outbreak patterns in wards; reporting notifiable diseases
  • Immunization: Understanding vaccines — live attenuated, killed, toxoid
  • Laboratory Liaison: Interpreting culture and sensitivity reports for doctors

Koch's Postulates (Important for Exams)

  • The pathogen must be present in all cases of disease
  • It must be isolated and grown in pure culture
  • Inoculation of pure culture must cause the disease in healthy host
  • The pathogen must be re-isolated from the experimentally infected host

Methods of Identification of Microorganisms
Unit II · Long Answer
▾

I. Microscopic Methods

  • Light microscopy: Most common; magnifies up to 1000× with oil immersion
  • Dark field microscopy: For spirochaetes (Treponema pallidum) that can't be stained
  • Phase contrast: Live unstained specimens; sees internal structures
  • Fluorescence microscopy: Uses fluorescent dyes (auramine-rhodamine for TB)
  • Electron microscopy: Visualize viruses; ultrafine structure; TEM & SEM

II. Staining Methods

  • Simple staining: Single dye; shows shape and arrangement
  • Gram staining: Differentiates G+ve and G-ve bacteria
  • Acid-fast staining (ZN stain): Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • Spore staining (Schaeffer-Fulton): Detects endospores — green spores, red vegetative cells
  • Capsule staining (Quellung/India ink): Negative staining — Cryptococcus, Klebsiella
  • Flagella staining: Leifson's method — visualizes flagella

III. Culture Methods

  • Nutrient agar / Broth: General growth media
  • Selective & Differential media: CLED, MacConkey, TCBS, Blood agar
  • Colony morphology: Size, shape, color, hemolysis — preliminary ID

IV. Biochemical Tests

  • IMViC tests: Indole, Methyl red, Voges-Proskauer, Citrate — identifies Enterobacteriaceae
  • Catalase test: Differentiates Staphylococcus (+) from Streptococcus (−)
  • Coagulase test: Identifies S. aureus (coagulase +ve)
  • Oxidase test: Pseudomonas (positive)
  • Sugar fermentation: Identifies organisms by fermentation pattern

V. Serological / Immunological Methods

  • Agglutination: Widal test (Typhoid), ASO titre (Strep)
  • ELISA: HIV, Hepatitis B, Dengue
  • Western Blot: Confirmatory for HIV
  • Complement fixation test: VDRL for syphilis
  • Latex agglutination: Rapid antigen detection

VI. Molecular Methods

  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Amplifies specific DNA; gold standard for many diseases
  • RT-PCR: For RNA viruses (SARS-CoV-2, HIV)
  • DNA sequencing / 16S rRNA: Definitive species identification
  • CBNAAT (GeneXpert): Rapid TB + rifampicin resistance detection

VII. Animal Inoculation

  • Used when organism cannot be cultured on media
  • Viruses (embryonated eggs), Toxin testing (mice), M. leprae (armadillo footpad)

VIII. Antibiogram (Sensitivity Testing)

  • Kirby-Bauer Disc Diffusion: Standard antibiotic susceptibility
  • MIC (Minimum Inhibitory Concentration): Broth dilution

Gram Staining & Acid-Fast Staining
Unit II · Most Important
▾

Gram Staining — Principle

  • Developed by Hans Christian Gram (1884)
  • Based on differences in cell wall peptidoglycan thickness
  • G+ve: Thick peptidoglycan → retains crystal violet → PURPLE
  • G-ve: Thin PG + outer lipopolysaccharide membrane → decolorized → counterstained → PINK/RED

Gram Staining — Steps (PVSI)

  • Step 1 – Primary stain: Apply Crystal Violet (1 min) → smear turns purple
  • Step 2 – Mordant: Apply Gram's Iodine (1 min) → forms crystal violet-iodine complex; mordant fixes the stain
  • Step 3 – Decolorization: Acetone-alcohol (10–20 sec) → G+ve retain purple; G-ve lose colour
  • Step 4 – Counterstain: Safranin or Neutral Red (1 min) → G-ve stain pink/red
  • Step 5 – Wash, dry, examine under oil immersion (100×)
🧠 Gram Stain Steps: "Please Don't Cry Sweetly" P=Primary stain (Crystal Violet), D=Decolorizer, C=Counterstain (Safranin) — or remember "Crystal Iodine Acetone Safranin" = CIAS

Gram Results & Examples

Acid-Fast Staining (Ziehl-Neelsen Stain) — Principle

  • Used to identify Mycobacterium species (TB, leprosy)
  • Mycobacteria have cell walls rich in mycolic acids (lipid-rich, waxy)
  • Resist decolorization with acid-alcohol → called "acid-fast" bacilli (AFB)
  • AFB appear red; non-acid-fast organisms appear blue (background)

ZN Staining — Steps

  • Step 1 – Primary stain: Carbol fuchsin (red dye) applied; smear heated with flame (until steaming — 3×5 min) to drive dye into waxy wall
  • Step 2 – Decolorization: 20% H₂SO₄ (acid-alcohol) — non-AFB decolorize; AFB remain red
  • Step 3 – Counterstain: Malachite green or Methylene blue (1–2 min) — background turns blue
  • Examine: AFB = bright red rods on blue background

Cold Method (Kinyoun's Stain)

  • No heating required — carbol fuchsin used at room temperature with higher concentrations
  • Suitable when heat application is not possible

Grading of AFB Smears (WHO/RNTCP)

  • Negative: No AFB seen in 100 fields
  • 1+ : 1–9 AFB/100 fields
  • 2+ : 10–99 AFB/100 fields
  • 3+ : 1–10 AFB/field
  • Scanty: 1–9 AFB in 100 fields — report exact number

Organisms That Are Acid-Fast

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB)
  • Mycobacterium leprae (leprosy)
  • Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC)
  • Nocardia (weakly acid-fast)
  • Cryptosporidium oocysts (parasitology)

Hanging Drop Preparation: Procedure
Unit II · Practical Procedure
▾

Purpose

  • Used to study motility of living bacteria in wet preparations
  • Also used to study Brownian motion, size, shape of organisms
  • Differentiates true motility (purposeful movement) from Brownian motion (random vibration)

Requirements

  • Hollow-ground (concave) slide or cavity slide
  • Cover slip
  • Petroleum jelly (Vaseline) or paraffin wax
  • Broth culture of bacteria (6–8 hour culture for maximum motility)
  • Wire loop, microscope

Procedure — Step by Step

  • Step 1: Apply a ring of petroleum jelly to the four edges of a clean cover slip using a cotton swab
  • Step 2: Place a small loopful (10 µL) of fresh broth culture in the centre of the cover slip
  • Step 3: Invert the cavity slide so the concavity faces downward and press gently onto the cover slip
  • Step 4: Turn the assembly upright quickly — the drop now hangs down into the concavity
  • Step 5: Seal edges with petroleum jelly to prevent drying
  • Step 6: Place on microscope stage; focus with low power (10×), then switch to high power (40×)
  • Step 7: Observe — the drop should be visible at the edge; bacteria appear as small refractile bodies
  • Do NOT use oil immersion (100×) as the cover slip may be damaged

Observations & Interpretation

  • True motility: Bacteria move in definite directions, change direction purposefully — organism is MOTILE
  • Brownian motion: All particles (including non-living) show random vibration due to molecular bombardment — does NOT indicate motility
  • Non-motile bacteria: Only Brownian motion seen; organisms drift with fluid currents

Motile vs Non-Motile Examples

  • Motile: Vibrio cholerae (darting motility — most characteristic), E. coli, Salmonella, Proteus
  • Non-motile: Klebsiella, Shigella, Staphylococcus, Streptococcus

Advantages & Limitations

  • ✅ Simple, inexpensive, rapid; uses live organisms; no staining needed
  • ✅ Can see actual movement in real time
  • ❌ Risk of infection — requires biohazard precautions
  • ❌ Cannot identify organisms; only motility status
  • ❌ Difficult to distinguish Brownian motion from very sluggish motility

Culture Media: Types & Uses
Unit III · Long Answer
▾

Definition

  • Culture media = Nutrient preparations used to grow microorganisms in laboratory
  • Must provide carbon, nitrogen, mineral salts, water, and growth factors

Classification by Physical State

  • Liquid / Broth media: No solidifying agent; used for large volumes, blood culture, MIC — e.g., Nutrient broth, Peptone water
  • Solid media: Contains 2% agar (seaweed polysaccharide); used for colony isolation — e.g., Nutrient agar
  • Semi-solid media: 0.5% agar; used for motility testing and antigen preservation — e.g., Hugh-Leifson media

Classification by Composition & Purpose

  • Simple/Basal media: Nutrient agar, peptone water — supports most organisms; used as a base for enriched media
  • Enriched media: Added blood, serum, eggs; for fastidious organisms — Blood agar (fastidious + shows hemolysis), Chocolate agar (Neisseria, Haemophilus), Löwenstein-Jensen (LJ) for M. tuberculosis
  • Selective media: Contains inhibitory agents to suppress unwanted flora; allows specific organisms to grow
    • MacConkey agar → G-ve Enterobacteriaceae (inhibits G+ve)
    • TCBS → Vibrio cholerae (yellow colonies)
    • CLED agar → Urinary pathogens
    • Thayer-Martin → Neisseria gonorrhoeae
    • Mannitol Salt Agar → Staphylococcus
  • Differential media: Differentiates organisms by biochemical reactions based on colony colour/appearance
    • MacConkey → Lactose fermenters (pink) vs non-fermenters (colourless)
    • Blood agar → α, β, γ hemolysis pattern
    • XLD agar → Salmonella (pink with black centers) vs others
  • Enrichment media: Broth media favouring a specific organism over others — Alkaline peptone water (Vibrio), Selenite F broth (Salmonella), Tetrathionate broth (Salmonella/Shigella)
  • Transport media: Preserve viability during transit — Stuart's medium (common), VR (Vibrio), Pike's (Streptococcus), Amies medium
  • Anaerobic media: Robertson's Cooked Meat medium, Thioglycollate broth
  • Indicator media: Contains pH indicators — MacConkey (neutral red), CLED (bromothymol blue)

Hemolysis on Blood Agar

  • α-hemolysis (partial): Green discolouration — Streptococcus viridans, S. pneumoniae
  • β-hemolysis (complete): Clear zone — S. pyogenes (Group A Strep), S. aureus
  • γ-hemolysis (no hemolysis): Enterococcus faecalis
🧠 Key media memory: MacConkey = Mac-Pink for lactose fermenters | TCBS = TC-Yellow for Vibrio | LJ = TB grows | Blood agar = hemolysis pattern | Thayer-Martin = Neisseria

Growth Curve of Bacteria & Phases
Unit III · Graph Question
▾

Definition

  • When bacteria are inoculated into fresh liquid medium, their growth follows a characteristic pattern called the bacterial growth curve
  • It is plotted as log of number of viable cells vs time
  • Binary fission is the primary mode of reproduction: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8... (exponential)

4 Phases of Bacterial Growth

  • Phase 1 — Lag Phase:
    • No increase in cell number
    • Bacteria adapt to new environment; synthesize enzymes, proteins, and RNA
    • Preparation for division; metabolically active but not dividing
    • Duration depends on age of culture and composition of medium
  • Phase 2 — Log (Exponential) Phase:
    • Rapid, geometric (exponential) increase in cell number
    • Cells are most metabolically active, uniform in size and staining
    • Most sensitive to antibiotics and antimicrobials
    • Doubling time is constant and at its minimum here
    • Best phase for antibiotic studies and vaccine production
  • Phase 3 — Stationary Phase:
    • Growth rate equals death rate → population remains constant
    • Nutrients depleted; waste/toxins accumulate; pH changes
    • Endospore formation occurs here (Bacillus, Clostridium)
    • Bacteria may produce secondary metabolites, exotoxins, and antibiotics
  • Phase 4 — Decline (Death) Phase:
    • Death rate exceeds growth rate; viable count falls sharply
    • Complete nutrient depletion; lethal accumulation of toxic metabolic products
    • Some cells may remain viable for long periods (survivors)

Doubling (Generation) Time

  • Time taken for the population to double
  • E. coli: ~20 minutes under optimal conditions
  • M. tuberculosis: ~18 hours (explains slow disease progression)

Clinical Significance

  • Antibiotics act best in log phase when bacteria are actively dividing
  • Stationary phase bacteria may be more antibiotic-resistant
  • M. TB slow growth explains need for prolonged ATT (6–9 months)
🧠 Phases: "Lazy Logs Stall & Die" L=Lag, L=Log, S=Stationary, D=Decline/Death

Factors Affecting Microbial Growth
Unit III · Important
▾

A. Physical Factors

  • Temperature:
    • Psychrophiles: −5 to 15°C (cold-loving) — Listeria
    • Mesophiles: 20–45°C (optimal 37°C) — most pathogens including E. coli, S. aureus
    • Thermophiles: 50–70°C — Bacillus stearothermophilus (used in autoclave testing)
    • Hyperthermophiles: >80°C — Archaea
  • pH:
    • Neutrophiles: pH 6.5–7.5 — most bacteria
    • Acidophiles: pH <5.5 — Lactobacillus, Helicobacter pylori
    • Alkaliphiles: pH >8.5 — Vibrio cholerae (prefers pH 8–9)
  • Osmotic pressure: Halophiles need high salt (Vibrio parahaemolyticus); most bacteria prefer isotonic conditions
  • Moisture/Water activity (aw): Most bacteria need aw >0.90; fungi can tolerate lower aw (drier conditions)
  • Light: UV light is bactericidal; most pathogens prefer darkness

B. Chemical Factors

  • Oxygen: Aerobes, anaerobes, facultative anaerobes, microaerophiles (as classified above)
  • Carbon dioxide: Some organisms are capnophiles (need 5–10% CO₂) — Neisseria, Brucella; use candle jar or CO₂ incubator
  • Nutrients: Carbon source, nitrogen, mineral salts (S, P, Mg, Fe), vitamins and growth factors required
  • Growth factors: Haemophilus needs Factor X (hemin) + Factor V (NAD); can't grow on simple agar

C. Biological Factors

  • Antibiosis: Inhibition by antibiotics or products of other microbes
  • Competition: Normal flora compete with pathogens; disruption leads to opportunistic infections
  • Bacteriophages: Viruses of bacteria — can limit bacterial growth

Nursing Implications

  • Refrigeration slows but doesn't kill organisms — food safety relevance
  • Proper temperature and humidity control in wards reduces pathogen survival
  • Antiseptics alter pH and chemical environment, inhibiting growth

Effects of Heat & Cold on Microorganisms
Unit IV · Theory
▾

Effects of Heat

  • Mechanism: Heat denatures proteins and enzymes, disrupts membranes, and causes coagulation of cellular proteins
  • Thermal Death Point (TDP): Lowest temperature that kills all organisms in a suspension in 10 minutes
  • Thermal Death Time (TDT): Minimum time to kill all organisms at a given temperature
  • D-value (Decimal Reduction Time): Time to reduce viable count by 90% at a specific temperature

Moist Heat Methods

  • Boiling (100°C): Kills vegetative bacteria, viruses, fungi in 10–30 min; does NOT kill spores
  • Autoclaving (121°C / 15 lbs/sq in / 15 min): Kills ALL including spores; most reliable sterilization method; uses saturated steam under pressure
  • Pasteurization:
    • HTST: 71.7°C for 15 seconds — kills pathogens in milk without affecting taste
    • LTLT: 63°C for 30 minutes
    • UHT: 135°C for 2 seconds — sterile milk (long shelf life)
  • Tyndallization (Fractional sterilization): 100°C for 30 min on 3 consecutive days — kills spores that germinate between sessions; used for heat-sensitive media
  • Inspissation: 80°C for 1 hour on 3 days — coagulates egg-containing media like LJ medium

Dry Heat Methods

  • Hot Air Oven (Red Heat / Flaming): 160°C for 1 hour OR 180°C for 30 min; for glassware, metal, oils, powders (items that can't be autoclaved)
  • Incineration: Complete burning; for pathological waste, cadavers, contaminated disposables
  • Flaming: Passing over Bunsen burner flame; for inoculation loops
  • Red heat: Holding metal objects in flame till red-hot

Comparison: Moist vs Dry Heat

MechanismProtein coagulationProtein oxidation
Temperature neededLowerHigher

Effects of Cold

  • Cold is bacteriostatic — slows/stops metabolism but generally does NOT kill bacteria
  • Refrigeration (4°C): Slows growth; used to preserve specimens and vaccines; Listeria and Yersinia can grow even at 4°C
  • Freezing (−20°C): Stops all growth; ice crystal formation damages cells; not used for routine sterilization
  • Deep freezing (−70°C): Used for long-term preservation of cultures, blood products, and vaccines
  • Lyophilization (Freeze-drying): Freezing + vacuum drying; excellent for long-term preservation of vaccines and organisms (no ice crystals)
  • Exceptions: Some bacteria like Vibrio cholerae die rapidly at cold temperatures

Hospital-Acquired Infections (HAI)
Unit IV · Most Important
▾

Definition

  • Also called Nosocomial infections or Healthcare-Associated Infections (HCAIs)
  • Infection acquired in a hospital or healthcare facility that was NOT present or incubating at the time of admission
  • Typically manifest ≥48 hours after admission (or within 30 days of healthcare contact)

Epidemiology

  • Affect ~7–10% of hospitalized patients globally
  • Leading cause of morbidity, mortality, and increased hospital stay/costs
  • ICU patients are at highest risk

Common Types (CAUTI, CLABSI, VAP, SSI)

  • UTI (most common HAI — ~40%): Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI); E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas
  • Surgical Site Infection (SSI): Post-operative wound infection; S. aureus (MRSA), Gram-negatives
  • Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP): Pneumonia in intubated patients; Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, Klebsiella
  • Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI): Central line associated; S. aureus, CoNS, Candida
  • C. difficile infection (CDI): Post-antibiotic diarrhea; pseudomembranous colitis
  • MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus): Major multidrug-resistant nosocomial pathogen

Sources & Reservoir

  • Endogenous: Patient's own flora (most common) — normal flora becomes pathogenic in immunocompromised
  • Exogenous: Healthcare workers (hands!), equipment, water/air systems, other patients
  • Common reservoirs: Sinks, IV lines, ventilators, urinary catheters, hands of HCWs

Mode of Transmission

  • Contact (most common): Direct (patient-to-patient) or indirect (contaminated hands/equipment)
  • Droplet: Influenza, RSV, Meningococcal
  • Airborne: TB, Chickenpox, Measles
  • Common vehicle: Contaminated IV fluids, food, blood products
  • Vector-borne: Less common in hospital settings

Risk Factors

  • Invasive devices: Urinary catheters, IV lines, ventilators, NG tubes
  • Surgical procedures; burns; immunocompromise (HIV, cancer, steroid use)
  • Extreme ages (neonates, elderly); prolonged hospitalization
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics → disruption of normal flora + selection of resistant organisms

Prevention — Bundle Approach

  • Hand Hygiene: WHO 5 moments — most effective single intervention; ABHR preferred; soap+water for C. difficile
  • CAUTI Bundle: Insert only when necessary; maintain closed drainage; daily review of catheter need; aseptic insertion; secure catheter
  • VAP Bundle: Head of bed elevation 30–45°; daily sedation breaks; oral care with chlorhexidine; cuff pressure monitoring
  • CLABSI Bundle: Hand hygiene; maximal barrier precautions; chlorhexidine skin prep; optimal site selection; daily review
  • SSI Prevention: Preoperative skin prep; prophylactic antibiotics; normothermia; sterile technique
  • Isolation precautions: Standard, contact, droplet, airborne as applicable
  • Antimicrobial stewardship: Rational antibiotic use; avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Surveillance: Active monitoring for HAI rates; microbiological environmental sampling

Role of Nurse in HAI Prevention

  • Strictly follow hand hygiene — before and after every patient contact
  • Perform aseptic technique for all invasive procedures
  • Daily assessment of necessity of invasive devices
  • Proper waste segregation (biohazard) and linen management
  • Patient and visitor education about hand hygiene
  • Report HAI trends to Infection Control Committee

Sterilization Methods: Physical, Chemical, Mechanical
Unit IV · Long Answer
▾

Definitions

  • Sterilization: Complete destruction/removal of ALL microorganisms including spores
  • Disinfection: Reduces/destroys pathogenic organisms but NOT spores
  • Antisepsis: Disinfection applied to living tissue (skin, mucous membranes)
  • Sanitization: Reduces microbial count to safe levels (used for food equipment)
  • Decontamination: Removal of contamination including chemical hazards

I. Physical Methods

  • Heat (most reliable):
    • Autoclave: 121°C, 15 psi, 15–20 min — gold standard; surgical instruments, dressings, media
    • Hot Air Oven: 160°C/1hr or 180°C/30min — glassware, oils, powders
    • Incineration — biological waste
    • Tyndallization — heat-sensitive culture media
  • Radiation:
    • UV radiation (254 nm): Non-ionizing; damages DNA (thymine dimers); used for air disinfection in OTs, labs; poor penetration
    • Gamma rays (ionizing): Cobalt-60; used for industrial sterilization of single-use medical devices, sutures, implants; excellent penetration
    • X-rays and electron beam: Industrial use
  • Desiccation/Drying: Removes water; bacteriostatic; used for food preservation
  • Ultrasonic waves: Disrupts cells; used in laboratory equipment cleaning

II. Chemical Methods

  • Glutaraldehyde (2%): High-level disinfectant/cold sterilant; endoscopes (20 min high-level; 3–10 hr sterilization); broad spectrum including spores
  • Formaldehyde (formalin): Sterilization of rooms (fumigation), preservation of specimens; toxic, carcinogenic
  • Ethylene oxide (ETO gas): Low-temperature gaseous sterilization; for heat-sensitive items — electronics, plastics, catheters; toxic, flammable; requires aeration period
  • Hydrogen peroxide (3–6%): Antiseptic/disinfectant; vapour phase used for equipment; rapid oxidizing action
  • Hypochlorite (bleach): 0.5–1% for surfaces; 0.05% for drinking water; effective against HIV, HBV, C. difficile spores
  • Iodine / Iodophors (Betadine): Skin antisepsis; broad spectrum; iodophors less irritating
  • Chlorhexidine: Pre-op skin prep; broad spectrum; residual activity; preferred over povidone-iodine in CLABSI bundles
  • Alcohol (70% ethanol/isopropanol): Rapid handwash; denatures proteins; NOT effective against spores; evaporates quickly
  • Phenol/phenolics: Lister's original antiseptic; Lysol for floor/environment disinfection

III. Mechanical Methods

  • Filtration:
    • Membrane filters (0.22 µm): Remove bacteria from heat-sensitive fluids (IV solutions, antibiotics, serum)
    • HEPA filters (0.3 µm): Remove 99.97% of particles; used in laminar flow hoods, OT HVAC, isolation rooms
    • Berkefeld / Chamberland filters (old): Candle-type; study of filterable viruses
  • Ultracentrifugation: Separates organisms from fluids; laboratory use

Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)
Unit V · Long Answer
▾

Causative Organism

  • Mycobacterium leprae — discovered by Armauer Hansen in 1873; called "Hansen's bacillus"
  • Obligate intracellular organism; cannot be cultured on artificial media
  • Acid-fast bacillus (AFB); arranged in bundles called "globi"
  • Slowest growing bacterial pathogen (doubling time ~13 days)
  • Animal model: Armadillo footpad; mouse footpad

Epidemiology

  • One of the oldest known diseases; India has highest prevalence globally
  • Source: Nasal secretions of untreated patients (most infective); skin lesions
  • Mode: Droplet inhalation; prolonged close contact; skin-to-skin (rare)
  • Incubation period: 2–5 years (range: 6 months–10 years)

Classification (Ridley-Jopling)

  • Tuberculoid (TT): Strong immunity; few lesions; AFB negative; hypopigmented anaesthetic macules
  • Lepromatous (LL): Weak immunity; many lesions; AFB positive (3+); leonine facies; glove and stocking anaesthesia
  • Borderline (BT, BB, BL): Intermediate forms

WHO Classification (for Treatment)

  • Paucibacillary (PB): 1–5 skin lesions; AFB smear negative — TT, BT
  • Multibacillary (MB): >5 skin lesions; AFB smear positive — BB, BL, LL

Pathogenesis

  • Infects peripheral nerves (Schwann cells) and skin macrophages
  • Targets cooler parts of body: skin, peripheral nerves, anterior eye, testes, upper respiratory mucosa
  • Nerve damage → anaesthesia, muscle weakness, deformities
  • Thickened peripheral nerves: Ulnar, radial cutaneous, common peroneal, great auricular, posterior tibial

Clinical Features

  • Hypopigmented or reddish skin patches with loss of sensation
  • Thickened peripheral nerves (palpable)
  • Claw hand (ulnar nerve), foot drop (peroneal), lagophthalmos (facial nerve)
  • Leonine facies (LL type) — thickening of facial skin
  • Lepra reactions: Type 1 (reversal) and Type 2 (Erythema Nodosum Leprosum — ENL)

Diagnosis

  • Clinical triad: Skin lesion + nerve enlargement + AFB in smear
  • Slit-skin smear (AFB): Ear lobules, chin, forehead, lesion edges
  • Lepromin test (Mitsuda test): Skin test; measures immune response; NOT diagnostic; determines type (positive in TT, negative in LL)
  • Skin biopsy: Histopathology

Treatment (MDT — Multi-Drug Therapy)

  • PB (6 months): Dapsone + Rifampicin (monthly supervised)
  • MB (12 months): Dapsone + Rifampicin (monthly supervised) + Clofazimine daily and monthly

Prevention & Control

  • Early detection and treatment; MDT provided free by WHO/Government
  • BCG vaccination offers partial protection (~50%)
  • Health education; reduce stigma; rehabilitation of deformed patients
  • Leprosaria/community-based rehabilitation

Tuberculosis (TB)
Unit V · Most Important
▾

Causative Organism

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis — "Koch's bacillus"; discovered by Robert Koch in 1882
  • Obligate aerobe; intracellular pathogen; thick mycolic acid cell wall
  • Slow-growing (18-hour doubling time); slender, slightly curved AFB
  • Other species: M. bovis (cattle; from unpasteurized milk), M. africanum

Epidemiology

  • India has highest TB burden globally; leading infectious disease killer worldwide
  • Source: Infected humans (open cases — sputum AFB positive)
  • Mode: Airborne droplet nuclei (<5 µm); inhaled into alveoli
  • Incubation: 4–8 weeks (primary infection); reactivation can occur years later
  • Risk: HIV/AIDS (50× increased risk), malnutrition, diabetes, silicosis, immunosuppression

Pathogenesis

  • Primary TB: First infection; Ghon focus forms in mid-lung zone; draining lymph nodes enlarged; Ghon complex = Ghon focus + lymph nodes; usually self-limiting
  • Primary progressive: In immunocompromised — spreads → miliary TB, meningitis
  • Post-primary (Reactivation) TB: Reactivation of latent focus; upper lobe; cavitation; cough productive of AFB — highly infectious
  • Granuloma formation: Macrophages engulf bacilli → epithelioid cells → Langhan's giant cells → granuloma → central caseous necrosis

Clinical Features

  • Constitutional: Fever (evening rise), night sweats, weight loss, fatigue (≥2 weeks)
  • Pulmonary: Chronic cough ≥2 weeks, haemoptysis, chest pain
  • Extrapulmonary: TB lymphadenitis (most common extra-pulmonary), TB meningitis, renal TB, skeletal TB (Pott's spine), abdominal TB

Diagnosis

  • Sputum AFB smear: Rapid, cheap; 3 samples (2 spot + 1 early morning)
  • CBNAAT (GeneXpert MTB/RIF): Detects M. tuberculosis AND rifampicin resistance in 2 hours; first-line test in India (NTEP)
  • Culture (LJ medium): Gold standard; takes 4–8 weeks; used for DST
  • Mantoux test (TST): Intradermal PPD; read at 48–72 hrs; ≥10 mm = positive (≥5 mm in HIV); tests previous exposure, NOT active disease
  • Chest X-ray: Upper lobe infiltrates, cavities, fibrosis
  • IGRA (Interferon Gamma Release Assay): QuantiFERON; more specific than Mantoux; for latent TB
  • TrueNat: Chip-based nucleic acid amplification; point-of-care

Treatment (NTEP Regimens)

  • Drug-Susceptible TB: 2HRZE (Intensive) + 4HR (Continuation) = 6 months total; HINH, Rifampicin, ZPyrazinamide, Ethambutol
  • Drug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB): Resistant to at least H + R; treated with longer regimens (18–24 months) with second-line drugs; Bedaquiline, Linezolid, Clofazimine
  • Directly Observed Treatment Short-course (DOTS): Core strategy of NTEP; ensures adherence

Prevention

  • BCG vaccine: Given at birth; protects against severe childhood TB (miliary, meningitis)
  • DOTS; early case detection and treatment
  • Contact tracing; preventive therapy for contacts (6H)
  • Airborne isolation for hospitalised open TB cases (negative pressure room, N95 mask)
  • Nikshay portal — digital patient tracking (NTEP India)
  • TB-Free India target: Eliminate TB by 2025 (National target)
🧠 TB drugs: "RIPE" = Rifampicin, INH, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol

Disease Processes Caused by Microorganisms
Unit V · Theory
▾

Steps in Disease Process (Chain of Infection)

  • 1. Infectious Agent: Pathogen with sufficient virulence, infectivity, and pathogenicity
  • 2. Reservoir: Natural habitat — humans, animals, soil, water, food
  • 3. Portal of Exit: Respiratory tract, GI tract, GU tract, skin/wound, blood
  • 4. Mode of Transmission: Contact, droplet, airborne, vector-borne, vehicle-borne
  • 5. Portal of Entry: Respiratory, GI, GU, skin, conjunctiva, placenta
  • 6. Susceptible Host: Determined by age, immunity, nutritional status, underlying disease

Virulence Factors

  • Adhesins: Pili, surface proteins — allow attachment to host cells
  • Toxins:
    • Exotoxins: Secreted proteins; highly potent; heat-labile; tetanus toxin, cholera toxin, diphtheria toxin
    • Endotoxins: LPS of G-ve cell wall; heat-stable; released on death; causes fever, septic shock
  • Enzymes: Hyaluronidase (spreading factor), streptokinase, coagulase, collagenase
  • Capsule: Anti-phagocytic — Klebsiella, Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Intracellular survival: Mycobacteria, Salmonella survive inside macrophages
  • Biofilm formation: Protects from antibiotics and phagocytes

Types of Infection

  • Primary infection: First infection in a healthy host
  • Secondary infection: Infection on top of existing disease
  • Latent infection: Organism persists dormant — HSV, TB, VZV
  • Opportunistic infection: Infections in immunocompromised — PCP in AIDS, CMV retinitis
  • Subclinical (inapparent) infection: No symptoms but serology positive
  • Carrier state: Person harbours organism without symptoms — Typhoid (Mary Mallon)
  • Acute, Subacute, Chronic infections classified by duration

Systemic Mycoses Including Histoplasmosis
Unit VI · Long Answer
▾

Definition

  • Systemic mycoses = deep fungal infections that involve internal organs; usually begin in lungs and disseminate
  • Often opportunistic in immunocompromised; some (dimorphic fungi) affect healthy hosts

Dimorphic Fungi (Important Category)

  • Exist as moulds at room temperature (25°C) and yeasts at body temperature (37°C)
  • Mnemonic: "B H C P S" — Blastomyces, Histoplasma, Coccidioides, Paracoccidioides, Sporothrix

Histoplasmosis

  • Organism: Histoplasma capsulatum
  • Source: Soil enriched with bird/bat droppings (caves, chicken coops); bird excreta
  • Transmission: Inhalation of microconidia (NOT person-to-person)
  • Distribution: Ohio and Mississippi River valleys (USA); worldwide tropical/subtropical
  • Pathogenesis: Inhaled spores → engulfed by macrophages → yeasts multiply inside macrophages → granuloma formation; resembles TB
  • Clinical forms:
    • Asymptomatic (90%)
    • Acute pulmonary: Flu-like illness; self-limiting
    • Chronic pulmonary: Resembles TB (upper lobe cavities)
    • Disseminated (in AIDS, immunocompromised): Fever, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, pancytopenia
  • Diagnosis: Culture (blood, bone marrow); histopathology (yeast inside macrophages — PAS stain); urinary antigen test (most sensitive in disseminated); serology
  • Treatment: Itraconazole (mild/moderate); Amphotericin B (severe/disseminated)

Other Systemic Mycoses

  • Cryptococcosis: Cryptococcus neoformans; pigeon droppings; capsulated yeast; meningitis in AIDS (most common fungal meningitis); India ink shows capsule; latex agglutination antigen test; treat with Ampho B + Flucytosine
  • Aspergillosis: Aspergillus fumigatus; inhalation of conidia; invasive aspergillosis in neutropenia; fungal ball (aspergilloma) in pre-existing cavity; allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA)
  • Candidiasis: Candida albicans; commonest opportunistic fungal infection; oral thrush, oesophagitis, vaginal, bloodstream (candidemia); germ tube test positive for C. albicans
  • Mucormycosis: Rhizopus, Mucor; "Black Fungus"; angioinvasive; rhinocerebral form in diabetics; COVID-19 association; treat with Ampho B + surgical debridement
  • Blastomycosis: Blastomyces dermatitidis; North America; lungs + skin + bone

Key Antifungal Drugs

  • Amphotericin B: Broad spectrum; binds ergosterol; for severe infections
  • Azoles (fluconazole, itraconazole, voriconazole): Inhibit ergosterol synthesis
  • Echinocandins (caspofungin): Inhibit cell wall glucan synthesis

Hypersensitivity: Types & Examples
Unit VII · Most Important
▾

Definition

  • Hypersensitivity = exaggerated or inappropriate immune response to an antigen (allergen) causing tissue damage
  • Classified by Gell and Coombs (1963) into 4 types

Type I — Immediate/Anaphylactic (IgE-mediated)

  • Mechanism: Allergen → IgE produced → binds to mast cells/basophils → re-exposure → cross-linking → degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins
  • Onset: Within minutes (15–30 min)
  • Cells: Mast cells, basophils, eosinophils; IgE antibody
  • Examples: Anaphylaxis (penicillin, bee sting), bronchial asthma, allergic rhinitis (hay fever), urticaria, food allergy (peanuts), atopic eczema
  • Treatment: Epinephrine (anaphylaxis emergency); antihistamines, corticosteroids; desensitization

Type II — Cytotoxic/Antibody-mediated (IgG/IgM)

  • Mechanism: Antibody binds to antigen on cell surface → complement activation or ADCC → cell destruction
  • Onset: Hours
  • Examples: Blood transfusion reactions (ABO incompatibility), hemolytic disease of newborn (Rh incompatibility), autoimmune hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture's syndrome (anti-GBM), Graves' disease (stimulatory), Myasthenia gravis

Type III — Immune Complex-mediated

  • Mechanism: Ag-Ab complexes deposited in tissues → complement activation → neutrophil infiltration → tissue damage
  • Onset: 6–12 hours
  • Examples: SLE (systemic lupus erythematosus), post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, serum sickness, farmer's lung (extrinsic allergic alveolitis), Arthus reaction (local)
  • Hallmark: Low complement (C3, C4) levels in serum

Type IV — Delayed-Type/Cell-mediated (T-cell mediated)

  • Mechanism: Sensitized T-lymphocytes (CD4+ Th1) → release cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF) → macrophage activation → granuloma formation; no antibody involved
  • Onset: 48–72 hours (delayed)
  • Examples: Contact dermatitis (nickel, poison ivy, latex gloves), Tuberculin/Mantoux reaction, granulomatous reactions (TB, leprosy, sarcoidosis), graft rejection, multiple sclerosis, Type 1 diabetes

Antigen-Antibody Reactions
Unit VII · Theory
▾

Definitions

  • Antigen (Ag): Any substance (protein, polysaccharide, lipid, nucleic acid) that can stimulate an immune response and react with specific antibody; usually foreign
  • Antibody (Ab) / Immunoglobulin (Ig): Glycoprotein produced by plasma cells in response to antigen; 5 classes: IgG, IgA, IgM, IgD, IgE
  • Epitope: Specific part of antigen that binds antibody (antigenic determinant)

Properties of Ag-Ab Reactions

  • Specificity: Antibody reacts only with its homologous antigen (lock and key)
  • Reversibility: Reaction is reversible; governed by affinity and avidity
  • Non-covalent bonds: Hydrogen bonds, Van der Waals, electrostatic, hydrophobic
  • Equivalent zone: Optimal ratio of Ag and Ab for maximum precipitation

Types of Ag-Ab Reactions (In Vitro Tests)

  • Agglutination: Antibody clumps particulate antigens (bacteria, RBCs) — Widal (typhoid), blood grouping, ABO typing
  • Precipitation: Soluble antigen + antibody → visible precipitate — Ouchterlony double diffusion
  • Complement fixation: Detects Ab by complement consumption — Wassermann for syphilis
  • Neutralization: Ab neutralizes toxins or viruses — ASO titre (anti-streptolysin O)
  • ELISA: Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay; very sensitive; used for HIV, HBsAg, dengue NS1
  • Western Blot: Confirmatory for HIV; detects specific protein bands
  • RIA (Radioimmunoassay): Uses radioactive labels; very sensitive
  • Immunofluorescence: Direct (DIF) and indirect (IIF); detects Ag or Ab in tissue sections
  • Coomb's test: Direct (detects Ab on RBCs) and Indirect (detects Ab in serum) — for hemolytic disease of newborn

Immunity: Types & Significance
Unit VII · Important
▾

Definition

  • Immunity = the ability of the body to resist disease-causing agents and their harmful products

Classification of Immunity

  • A. Innate (Non-Specific) Immunity:
    • Present at birth; not antigen-specific; no memory; immediate response
    • Physical barriers: Skin, mucous membranes, cilia, gastric acid
    • Chemical barriers: Lysozyme (tears, saliva), defensins, complement
    • Cellular: Phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), NK cells
    • Inflammatory response; fever
  • B. Acquired (Adaptive/Specific) Immunity:
    • Develops after exposure to antigen; specific; has memory; slower (days)
    • Active immunity: Body produces its own antibodies
      • Natural active: After infection (e.g., measles — lifelong immunity)
      • Artificial active: After vaccination (e.g., BCG, OPV, MMR)
    • Passive immunity: Ready-made antibodies received from outside
      • Natural passive: IgG crosses placenta; IgA in colostrum (first 3 months of life)
      • Artificial passive: Injection of antiserum/immunoglobulin (ATS for tetanus, HBIG for Hep B, rabies Ig)

Herd Immunity

  • When a sufficient proportion of a population is immune, it indirectly protects non-immune individuals
  • Threshold varies: Measles ~95%, Polio ~80–85%
  • Important for disease elimination and eradication programs

Types of Vaccines

  • Live attenuated: BCG, OPV, MMR, Varicella — strong long-lasting immunity; contraindicated in immunocompromised
  • Killed/Inactivated: IPV, Hepatitis A, whole-cell pertussis — safer; needs boosters
  • Toxoid: Inactivated toxins — TT, DT for tetanus and diphtheria
  • Subunit/Recombinant: HBsAg (Hepatitis B), HPV vaccine
  • Conjugate: Hib, PCV, MenACWY — polysaccharide conjugated to protein carrier; effective in infants

Definition of Parasites & Vectors
Unit VIII · Definitions
▾

Parasite

  • An organism that lives on or within another organism (host) from which it obtains nutrients, causing harm to the host
  • Host: Organism that harbours the parasite
  • Definitive host: Host in which sexual reproduction of parasite occurs (e.g., humans for Plasmodium in erythrocytic schizogony)
  • Intermediate host: Host for asexual stage — Anopheles mosquito for Plasmodium (sporogony)
  • Reservoir host: Animal that maintains parasite but is not the primary host — pigs for T. solium

Types of Parasites

  • Ectoparasite: Lives on surface — lice, ticks, mites, fleas
  • Endoparasite: Lives inside host — worms, protozoa
  • Obligate parasite: Cannot survive without host — Plasmodium, Leishmania
  • Facultative parasite: Can live as free-living or as parasite — Naegleria, Strongyloides
  • Accidental/incidental parasite: Infects an unusual host

Vector

  • A living carrier (usually arthropod) that transmits infectious agents from one host to another
  • Biological vector: Parasite undergoes multiplication/development inside vector — Anopheles (malaria), Aedes (dengue), Culex (filariasis)
  • Mechanical vector: Transmits pathogen without parasite development — housefly (typhoid, dysentery)

  • 2. Helminths (multicellular worms):
    • Nematodes (roundworms): Ascaris, Hookworm, Pinworm (Enterobius), Trichinella, Filaria (Wuchereria), Strongyloides
    • Trematodes (flukes): Schistosoma (blood fluke), Fasciola hepatica (liver fluke), Paragonimus (lung fluke)
    • Cestodes (tapeworms): Taenia solium (pork), T. saginata (beef), Echinococcus (hydatid cyst), Diphyllobothrium
  • 3. Ectoparasites (Arthropods):
    • Insects: Lice (Pediculus), Fleas (Pulex), Flies, Mosquitoes, Bugs
    • Arachnids: Ticks (Ixodes, Dermacentor), Mites (Sarcoptes scabiei), Spiders

Protozoal Infections
Unit VIII · Long Answer
▾

1. Malaria

  • Organism: Plasmodium vivax (benign tertian), P. falciparum (malignant tertian — most dangerous), P. malariae (quartan), P. ovale
  • Vector: Female Anopheles mosquito (dusk-to-dawn biter)
  • Life cycle: Sporozoites → liver (exo-erythrocytic schizogony) → merozoites → RBCs (erythrocytic schizogony) → trophozoites → schizonts → merozoites (fever) / gametocytes
  • Clinical: Classical triad — cold stage + hot stage + sweating stage; cyclic fever; splenomegaly; anaemia
  • P. falciparum complications: Cerebral malaria, blackwater fever (haemoglobinuria), severe anaemia, ARDS, renal failure
  • Diagnosis: Peripheral blood smear (thick + thin) — most important; RDT (rapid antigen test); PCR; Quantitative Buffy Coat (QBC)
  • Diagnosis on smear: Ring forms (all species); "Schüffner's dots" (P. vivax/ovale); "Maurer's clefts" (P. falciparum); banana-shaped gametocytes (P. falciparum)
  • Treatment: Chloroquine (P. vivax); ACT - Artemisinin Combination Therapy for P. falciparum; Primaquine for radical cure of P. vivax/ovale (kills hypnozoites)
  • Prevention: Vector control (insecticides, ITNs); chemoprophylaxis; indoor residual spraying

2. Amoebiasis

  • Organism: Entamoeba histolytica
  • Transmission: Faecal-oral; contaminated food/water; cysts are infective form
  • Disease: Amoebic dysentery (bloody mucoid stool, flask-shaped ulcers in colon); amoebic liver abscess (most common extra-intestinal — "anchovy sauce" aspirate)
  • Diagnosis: Stool microscopy (cysts, trophozoites with ingested RBCs — pathognomonic); serology (ELISA) for liver abscess; ultrasound
  • Treatment: Metronidazole (tissue amoebiasis); Diloxanide furoate (luminal cysts)

3. Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis)

  • Organism: Leishmania donovani
  • Vector: Female sandfly (Phlebotomus)
  • Features: Prolonged fever, massive splenomegaly, hepatomegaly, lymphadenopathy, anaemia, wasting (kala-azar = black fever)
  • Diagnosis: Splenic aspirate smear (most sensitive), bone marrow biopsy, rK39 antigen card test; LD bodies (amastigotes in macrophages)
  • Treatment: Liposomal Amphotericin B (first-line in India); Miltefosine

4. Giardiasis

  • Giardia lamblia — pear-shaped, bilateral symmetry ("falling leaf" motility); traveller's diarrhoea; malabsorption; steatorrhoea; cysts in stool (infective form)
  • Diagnosis: Stool microscopy (cysts/trophozoites); antigen detection
  • Treatment: Metronidazole/Tinidazole

5. Toxoplasmosis

  • Toxoplasma gondii — definitive host = cat; toxoplasmosis in pregnancy → congenital toxoplasmosis (TORCH); intracranial calcifications, retinochoroiditis, hydrocephalus
  • Diagnosis: Serology (IgM for acute); CT brain in AIDS (ring-enhancing lesions)
  • Treatment: Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine

Helminthic Infections (Short Notes)
Unit VIII · Short Notes
▾

1. Ascariasis (Roundworm)

  • Ascaris lumbricoides; largest intestinal nematode; faecal-oral transmission
  • Loeffler's syndrome (pulmonary eosinophilia) during larval migration; intestinal obstruction in heavy infection
  • Diagnosis: Stool microscopy (eggs); adult worm passed in stool/vomit
  • Treatment: Albendazole / Mebendazole

2. Hookworm (Ancylostomiasis)

  • Ancylostoma duodenale (Old World), Necator americanus (New World)
  • Larva penetrates skin (ground itch) → lungs → intestine; sucks blood → iron-deficiency anaemia
  • Diagnosis: Stool microscopy (eggs); eosinophilia
  • Treatment: Albendazole; iron supplementation

3. Pinworm (Enterobiasis)

  • Enterobius vermicularis; perianal itching (especially night — pruritus ani)
  • Diagnosis: Scotch tape test (morning, before bathing) — eggs on perianal skin
  • Treatment: Mebendazole/Albendazole; treat entire family

4. Tapeworm (Taeniasis)

  • Taenia solium (pork); Taenia saginata (beef)
  • Cysticercosis: When humans ingest T. solium eggs → larvae (cysticerci) in muscles, brain (neurocysticercosis — seizures), eyes
  • Diagnosis: Stool for proglottids/eggs; CT/MRI brain for neurocysticercosis
  • Treatment: Praziquantel; Albendazole + steroids for neurocysticercosis

5. Lymphatic Filariasis (Elephantiasis)

  • Wuchereria bancrofti; vector = Culex mosquito
  • Microfilariae circulate in blood (nocturnal periodicity); adult worms in lymphatics → lymphedema → elephantiasis of legs, scrotum; hydrocoele
  • Diagnosis: Midnight blood smear (microfilariae); ICT card test (antigen)
  • Treatment: DEC (Diethylcarbamazine) + Albendazole; MDA (Mass Drug Administration)

6. Schistosomiasis

  • Schistosoma mansoni (intestinal), S. haematobium (urinary — haematuria)
  • Transmission: Cercariae penetrate skin in freshwater (swimmer's itch)
  • Treatment: Praziquantel

Vector & Vector-Borne Diseases
Unit VIII · Applied
▾

Control of Vector-Borne Diseases

  • Environmental control: Eliminate breeding sites; drainage of stagnant water; waste management
  • Biological control: Introduce natural predators — Gambusia fish eats mosquito larvae; Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (Bti) — larvicide
  • Chemical control: Insecticides (DDT, malathion, deltamethrin); indoor residual spraying (IRS); space spraying
  • Personal protection: Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs/LLINs), repellents (DEET), protective clothing
  • Host-targeted: Vaccines (dengue vaccine, malaria vaccine RTS,S); chemoprophylaxis (chloroquine); mass drug administration (filariasis)

Key Vector Control Principles

  • Anopheles mosquito: Active at dusk/dawn; breeds in clean stagnant water; ITNs most effective
  • Aedes aegypti: Day biter; breeds in small collections of clear water (flower pots, tyres); dengue, Zika
  • Culex: Breeds in dirty water, drains; filariasis, JE
  • Sandfly: Nocturnal; breeds in soil, rubble; kala-azar; DDT IRS effective

Nursing Role in Vector Control

  • Educate community about breeding sites elimination (source reduction)
  • Promote use of bed nets; protective clothing; repellents
  • Ensure proper disposal of waste water; community surveillance
  • Participate in active fever case detection during outbreaks
  • Administer chemoprophylaxis and vaccines as part of prevention programs

Viral Properties & Classification
Unit IX · Theory
▾

Definition & Properties of Viruses

  • Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites — cannot replicate outside living host cells
  • Contain only ONE type of nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) — not both
  • No cell wall, no ribosomes, no metabolic enzymes of their own
  • Range: 20–300 nm (smaller than bacteria; visible only by electron microscopy)
  • Not killed by antibiotics; treated by specific antiviral drugs

Structure of Virus (Virion)

  • Nucleic acid (genome): DNA or RNA; single-stranded (ss) or double-stranded (ds)
  • Capsid: Protein coat surrounding nucleic acid; made of capsomeres; protects genome
  • Nucleocapsid: Nucleic acid + capsid
  • Envelope: Lipid bilayer (from host cell membrane) + glycoproteins; not present in all viruses; enveloped = sensitive to ether/detergent
  • Spikes: Surface glycoproteins used for attachment (e.g., Haemagglutinin and Neuraminidase of influenza)

Classification of Viruses

  • By nucleic acid type:
    • DNA viruses: Herpesviruses (HSV, VZV, CMV, EBV), Adenovirus, Papillomavirus (HPV), Poxvirus (smallpox), Hepatitis B
    • RNA viruses: Influenza, HIV, Hepatitis A/C/E, Rabies, Measles, Mumps, Rubella, Dengue, Polio, Rotavirus
  • By presence of envelope:
    • Enveloped: HIV, Influenza, HSV, Rabies, Hepatitis B/C — sensitive to detergents and drying
    • Non-enveloped (Naked): Poliovirus, Adenovirus, Rotavirus, Hepatitis A — more resistant to environment
  • By capsid symmetry: Icosahedral (spherical), Helical (tubular), Complex (poxvirus)
  • Baltimore Classification: Based on mRNA production strategy (Classes I–VII)

Viral Replication Cycle (APABRS)

  • Attachment (adsorption) to specific host cell receptor
  • Penetration (endocytosis or membrane fusion)
  • Uncoating (release of nucleic acid)
  • Biosynthesis (replication of nucleic acid + protein synthesis)
  • Assembly (maturation of new virions)
  • Release (budding for enveloped; lysis for non-enveloped)

Common Viral Diseases: Polio, Rabies, Hepatitis, AIDS
Unit IX · Most Important
▾

1. Poliomyelitis

  • Virus: Poliovirus — Enterovirus; ssRNA; 3 serotypes (1,2,3); type 1 most common cause of paralysis
  • Transmission: Faecal-oral route; waterborne
  • Pathogenesis: Replicates in gut → viraemia → motor neurons of anterior horn → irreversible flaccid paralysis
  • Clinical: Asymptomatic (90%); minor illness (fever, sore throat); non-paralytic aseptic meningitis; flaccid asymmetric paralysis of limbs (1%); bulbar polio
  • Diagnosis: Stool virus isolation (most important); CSF, serology
  • Vaccines: OPV (oral — Sabin; live attenuated; cheap; herd immunity; rare VAPP); IPV (Salk; killed; injected; no VAPP)
  • India declared polio-free in 2014

2. Rabies

  • Virus: Rabies lyssavirus — RNA; bullet-shaped; Rhabdoviridae
  • Transmission: Bite of infected animal (dog, bat, wolf); virus in saliva
  • Pathogenesis: Virus enters through wound → travels along peripheral nerves → brain → encephalitis → death; centrifugal spread to salivary glands, cornea, skin
  • Incubation: 10 days–1 year (average 1–3 months); longer if bite distal
  • Clinical: Prodrome (fever, pain at bite site) → Encephalitic (hydrophobia, aerophobia, photophobia, spasms, agitation) → Paralytic (ascending paralysis) → Coma → Death (99.9% fatal once symptomatic)
  • Negri bodies: Pathognomonic intracytoplasmic inclusions in hippocampal neurons; seen on post-mortem brain biopsy
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): Wound washing (soap + water 15 min → iodine); Rabies vaccine (5 doses: Day 0,3,7,14,28 — Essen schedule); Rabies immunoglobulin (RIG — infiltrated into wound) for category III exposure
  • Pre-exposure prophylaxis: For veterinarians, lab workers

4. HIV/AIDS

  • Virus: Human Immunodeficiency Virus — Lentivirus; ssRNA; two types: HIV-1 (worldwide), HIV-2 (West Africa)
  • Transmission: Unprotected sex (most common), blood transfusion/needles, mother-to-child (perinatal)
  • Pathogenesis: HIV binds CD4 receptor + CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptor → infects CD4+ T cells, macrophages, dendritic cells → reverse transcriptase converts RNA → DNA → integrates into host genome (provirus) → gradual CD4 cell destruction → immunodeficiency
  • Stages: Primary HIV infection (seroconversion illness) → Asymptomatic latent phase (years) → Symptomatic (PGL, oral thrush) → AIDS (CD4 <200 cells/µL)
  • AIDS-defining illnesses: PCP, CMV retinitis, Toxoplasmosis, Cryptococcal meningitis, TB, Kaposi's sarcoma, MAC
  • Diagnosis: ELISA (screening) → Western Blot (confirmatory); CD4 count; viral load (PCR)
  • Treatment — ART: Antiretroviral therapy; minimum 3 drugs; NRTIs + NNRTI or PI; suppresses viral load to undetectable; lifelong
  • Prevention: Condoms; clean needles (NSEP); blood screening; PMTCT; PrEP; PEP within 72 hours after exposure
  • India: NACO oversees AIDS control programme

General Principles of Viral Disease Control

  • 1. Vaccination (most effective):
    • Smallpox: First disease eradicated by vaccination (1980)
    • Polio: Near eradication; OPV + IPV campaigns
    • Measles, Mumps, Rubella: MMR vaccine

    • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu): Influenza A/B

    • DAAs (sofosbuvir, ledipasvir): Hepatitis C — curative

  • 3. Passive Immunization (post-exposure):
    • HRIG: Rabies post-exposure
    • HBIG: Hepatitis B post-exposure, newborns of HBsAg+ mothers

  • 4. Vector Control: Eliminate Aedes (dengue, Zika); Anopheles (arboviral)

  • Safe handling and disposal of sharps — never recap needles
  • Immediate PEP after needlestick (HIV, HBV)
  • OSHA guidelines and WHO standard precautions

  • Educate patients on safe sex, needle safety, blood exposure risks
  • Follow airborne/droplet/contact isolation precautions correctly
  • Report notifiable diseases (polio, measles, hepatitis, rabies)
  • Support ART adherence counselling for HIV patients
  • Manage needlestick injury protocol — wash, report, PEP within 2 hours

Food Poisoning: Explanation
Unit X · Long Answer
▾

Definition

  • Food poisoning = illness caused by ingestion of food or water containing pathogenic microorganisms, their toxins, or chemical poisons
  • Characterized by acute onset of GI symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps
  • Multiple people eating the same food fall ill simultaneously (point-source outbreak)

Classification

  • 1. Bacterial food poisoning (most common):
    • Staphylococcal FP: S. aureus enterotoxin (heat-stable); cooked meats, custard, dairy; vomiting within 1–6 hrs; self-limiting; no fever; MOST RAPID onset
    • Botulism: C. botulinum toxin (most potent); home-canned food; descending flaccid paralysis, diplopia, dysphagia; treat with antitoxin
    • Salmonella FP: Eggs, poultry, milk; 12–48 hrs; fever, diarrhoea, vomiting; treat with fluids ± antibiotics
    • Bacillus cereus: Two syndromes: emetic (fried rice, 1–5 hrs) and diarrhoeal (meats/vegetables, 8–16 hrs)
    • Clostridium perfringens: Cooked meat/poultry rewarmed; 8–12 hrs; diarrhoea, cramps; no vomiting; self-limiting
    • E. coli (EHEC/ETEC): ETEC (traveller's diarrhoea); EHEC O157:H7 (haemorrhagic colitis, HUS — haemolytic uremic syndrome)
    • Vibrio parahaemolyticus: Raw/undercooked seafood; coastal regions; diarrhoea, vomiting
    • Listeria monocytogenes: Unpasteurized dairy, deli meats; dangerous in pregnancy (crosses placenta) and elderly
  • 2. Viral food poisoning: Norovirus (most common cause of epidemic gastroenteritis), Rotavirus, Hepatitis A, Astrovirus
  • 3. Chemical food poisoning: Heavy metals (lead, mercury), pesticides, mycotoxins (Aflatoxin from Aspergillus flavus in peanuts/maize)

Investigation of Food Poisoning Outbreak

  • Collect history: Food eaten, time of onset, symptoms of all affected
  • Collect samples: Stool, vomitus, blood cultures from patients; food samples from kitchen
  • Notify health authorities; isolate contaminated food batch
  • Calculate attack rates; identify common food vehicle
  • Report to Surveillance Cell

Nurse's Role in Prevention of Food Poisoning
Unit X · Nursing Application
▾

Role in Patient Care

  • Assess and monitor hydration status; IV fluids for dehydration
  • Record food intake history and correlate with symptom onset
  • Collect and send appropriate specimens (stool, blood cultures, vomitus) before antibiotics
  • Maintain enteric precautions; isolation if required
  • Monitor for complications: Haemolytic uremic syndrome (EHEC), paralysis (botulism), electrolyte imbalance

Patient & Community Education (WHO 5 Keys)

  • 1. Keep CLEAN: Wash hands before handling food; wash fruits/vegetables; clean food preparation surfaces; prevent contamination from kitchen pests
  • 2. Separate RAW and COOKED: Avoid cross-contamination; separate cutting boards for meat and vegetables; store raw meat below cooked food in refrigerator
  • 3. COOK thoroughly: Cook meat, poultry, eggs, seafood to safe temperatures (70°C core); ensure juices run clear
  • 4. Keep food at SAFE temperatures: Don't leave cooked food at room temperature >2 hours; keep cold foods below 5°C (danger zone: 5–60°C)
  • 5. Use SAFE water and raw materials: Use clean/treated water; select fresh, seasonal food; avoid unpasteurized milk

Hospital Food Safety

  • Ensure hospital kitchen follows HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) principles
  • Monitor food handlers' health — screen for typhoid, TB, skin infections
  • Ensure proper refrigeration of ward diets; avoid batch cooking kept warm for hours
  • Report suspected foodborne outbreaks in ward immediately to infection control
  • Maintain cold chain for immunocompromised patients' special diets

Outbreak Response

  • Identify and isolate suspected food; stop serving; notify kitchen
  • Document all affected persons; collect stool samples urgently
  • Notify Medical Officer in Charge and Infection Control Nurse
  • Submit food samples to Public Health Laboratory
  • Assist in contact tracing and follow-up

Foodborne Infections: Causes & Prevention
Unit X · Short/Long
▾

Food Poisoning vs Foodborne Infection

  • Food poisoning (Intoxication): Caused by pre-formed toxin in food; short incubation; S. aureus, C. botulinum
  • Foodborne infection: Caused by ingesting viable organisms that then multiply and cause disease in the host; longer incubation; Salmonella, Shigella, Cholera, Hepatitis A, Typhoid

Important Foodborne Infections

  • Typhoid Fever (Enteric Fever):
    • Salmonella typhi; faecal-oral; contaminated water/food; incubation 1–3 weeks
    • Stepladder fever; rose spots on abdomen; relative bradycardia; splenomegaly
    • Widal test (O+H agglutinins); blood culture (1st week), stool/urine (2nd week)
    • Treatment: Azithromycin/Cephalosporins; chloramphenicol (historical)
  • Cholera:
    • Vibrio cholerae O1 (El Tor); faecal-oral; contaminated water
    • Rice-water stools (profuse, painless watery diarrhoea); rapid dehydration
    • Treatment: Rapid oral/IV rehydration (ORS); Doxycycline/Azithromycin
  • Hepatitis A: HAV; faecal-oral; contaminated shellfish; jaundice; self-limiting; vaccine available
  • Brucellosis: Brucella; unpasteurized milk/cheese; undulant fever; treat with Doxycycline + Rifampicin
  • Bacillary Dysentery: Shigella; faecal-oral; bloody diarrhoea (dysentery); HUS with S. dysenteriae; treat with Azithromycin/Ciprofloxacin

Prevention of Foodborne Infections

  • Safe water: Chlorination; boiling; filtration; WHO safe water standards
  • Food hygiene: Pasteurization of milk; proper cooking; refrigeration
  • Personal hygiene: Hand washing; food handler screening and vaccination
  • Environmental sanitation: Proper sewage disposal; fly control; garbage management
  • Vaccination: Typhoid vaccine (Vi polysaccharide); Hepatitis A vaccine; Cholera vaccine (OCV)
  • Food safety legislation: FSSAI (India) standards; HACCP; food labelling
  • Surveillance: Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP) for foodborne outbreak detection

Microbial Load in Food

  • Total viable count (TVC) — indicates freshness/contamination level
  • Coliform count — indicates faecal contamination
  • Safe limits: Drinking water must have 0 coliform/100 mL; food has variable limits by type
